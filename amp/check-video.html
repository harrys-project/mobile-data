Apple iPhone privacy battle felt in Knox County

story by: Rachel Wittel
Fri, 19 Feb 2016

(KNOXVILLE) Months after two terrorists killed 14 people in San Bernardino, Calif., the FBI is demanding Apple help them unlock one of the phones used by the attackers. 

A federal magistrate judge ordered Apple on Tuesday to create software that would disable the security feature that erases data from the iPhone after too many unsuccessful attempts to unlock it.

It's a privacy versus security issue that's affecting the nation down to a local level.

“We could unlock iPhones for a while, so we did have phones that were iPhones that we unlocked that we used in cases," Knox County District Attorney General Charme Allen said. "But since Apple has done this, we do have a collection of phones starting at law enforcement that we have seized that are sitting there that we cannot get into, so it has become an issue.”

The phones now idle are held in cases involving crimes against children, according to Allen.

Allen said phone data is key in solving many crimes, especially in child abuse, drug- or gun-related cases.

“As a district attorney, my charge is to keep folks safe," she said. "Anything that I can do to keep folks safer is what I’m going to pursue. When there’s information inside a phone that helps us solve a crime, that helps us prevent a crime, that helps us prosecute a crime, I’m going to go after that and want that information.”

Although she said access to phones is key, critics believe the FBI-Apple case is putting the average phone user's information at serious risk.

“Certainly there is a good interest in getting into this phone, particularly in San Bernardino," attorney Don Bosch said. "But the question is what about the next time? That may not be as good of a reason or as legitimate as a security interest as it might outweigh privacy concerns?"

Bosch said if Apple does provide the requested technical assistance, history could repeat itself in a negative way.

"While the FBI is claiming this is just one phone they want to look into, they need the proprietary information that Apple possesses to encrypt their phones," he said. "Once they possess it, they’re in a better position to use it or not, and unfortunately in our history, we’ve had far too many cases of law enforcement and abuses that have later been found to be inappropriate.”

Allen said it takes work to access a person's phone.

“To get into a person’s phone, I have to have a search warrant," she said. "Law enforcement can’t just go through anybody’s phone and invade your privacy for no reason. We don’t even get to the point to need to go into the phone until we can link it to a particular crime, so if there’s no criminal activity involved, we would never go into your phone.”

She also said if Apple makes the software, and it happens to get in the wrong hands down the road, law enforcement is there to help.

“If your rights are really violated and someone gets into your phone that doesn’t need to be in your phone, whether it’s the government or it's your next door neighbor," she said. "If it’s a crime, then we will prosecute that as well.”

Apple said late Tuesday it would oppose the ruling.
